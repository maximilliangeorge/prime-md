# What every picture must have in common with reality is the logical form

What the picture must have in common with reality in order to be able to
represent it after its manner — rightly or falsely — is its form of
representation. (2.17) The picture can represent every reality whose form it
has. (2.171)

But the picture cannot represent its form of representation; it shows it
forth. (2.172) The picture cannot place itself outside of its form of
representation. (2.174)

What every picture, of whatever form, must have in common with reality in order
to be able to represent it at all — rightly or falsely — is the logical form,
that is, the form of reality. (2.18) Every picture is also a logical
picture. (2.182) The logical picture can depict the world. (2.19)

This is the deepest constraint on representation: a picture must share the very
form it cannot itself depict.
